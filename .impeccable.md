## Design Context

### Users

Families and groups of friends playing a light-strategy math-rummy game together in the same physical space. Two to eight people each use their own phone or tablet, with one device hosting a local room and the others joining by code or QR over local Wi-Fi, hotspot, or Bluetooth discovery. The interface must make room setup, private hands, turn ownership, reconnection, and shared table state immediately understandable.

### Brand Personality

Playful, clever, and social. The experience should feel like a polished tabletop game rather than a classroom quiz: confident enough for adults, approachable for younger players, and lively without becoming childish.

### Aesthetic Direction

Follow the supplied “Progressive Math Card Game” screen reference. Use deep primary navy play surfaces, crisp warm-white card faces, action blue for primary controls, amber for active-turn emphasis, and clear green/red semantic states. Pair compact uppercase labels with bold geometric headings and highly legible body copy. Number cards should look and behave like a real playing-card deck. The home, room, and lobby screens are portrait-first; active play adapts deliberately between portrait and landscape. Avoid generic dashboard cards, neon gaming effects, school/test visual language, and decorative gradients unrelated to the felt table.

### Design Principles

1. Make the current game state and active player unmistakable using text, icons, and color together.
2. Preserve the tabletop metaphor: opponents around the felt, shared piles in the center, and the private hand anchored to the player.
3. Keep every important action reachable with a 44×44 minimum touch target and provide tap interactions alongside any drag behavior.
4. Adapt composition—not merely scale it—between portrait setup screens and landscape gameplay.
5. Design for unreliable local links: connection quality, ready state, held seats, and recovery timers remain visible and calm.
6. Keep arithmetic readable and operations explicit; operation controls are UI controls, never cards.
